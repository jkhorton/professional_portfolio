Strategic Frameworks

Building a Data & AI Function

While a data & AI strategy is important for every organization to have, it doesn't mean that a dedicated data and AI team is always needed. The right time to form a dedicated data and AI function in a company is when data begins to play a strategic role in achieving business goals.

Show More ▼

When to Form a Data & AI Function

  • Business Complexity & Scale: The organization has grown to a scale where data silos, manual analysis, and inconsistent metrics impede decision-making.
  • Strategic Use of AI: The company needs advanced capabilities like predictive analytics, real-time insights, or AI-driven automation for critical processes.
  • Rapid Growth or Digital Transformation: During rapid expansion or digital initiatives, the company needs to scale data collection, processing, and insight generation.
  • Regulatory and Compliance Requirements: The organization operates in industries with stringent data regulations (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA) requiring accurate reporting and data governance.

When NOT to Form One

  • Limited Data Use: The business operates with simple processes, small data volumes, or minimal reliance on data-driven decisions.
  • No Clear ROI from Data Investments: The cost of building a data and AI function outweighs the benefits due to unclear or insufficient use cases.
  • Misaligned Business Objectives: If the organization's strategic goals do not yet align with leveraging data as a competitive advantage.

Team Structure Examples

  • Lean Startup Model: For startups or smaller businesses beginning to use data for competitive advantage. Small, multi-functional team covering data engineering, analysis, and lightweight AI experimentation with outsourced expertise for specialized tasks.
  • Centralized: Ideal for organizations needing uniform data governance and centralized decision-making. Includes CDAO/CDAIO, Analytics Engineers, Data Scientists & AI Engineers, and Data Governance specialists.
  • Decentralized: Suitable for large organizations with diverse business units needing tailored insights. Similar roles embedded in individual business units with a small centralized team for governance.
  • Hub & Spoke: For companies balancing centralized control and business unit autonomy. Centralized team (Hub) for engineering, data science, governance, and AI innovation with embedded teams (Spokes) in business units.

Building a Data & AI Strategy

A strong data and AI strategy turns data into a strategic asset that drives smarter decisions, operational efficiency, and innovation. By aligning data practices with business goals, organizations can generate actionable insights, improve customer experiences, and gain a competitive edge.

Show More ▼

Key Components

Objectives & Use Cases

  • Vision & Goals: Define what the organization aims to achieve with data and AI
  • Use Cases: Identify specific, measurable, and high-impact use cases aligned with business goals

Technology & Infrastructure

  • Data Platforms: Leverage robust data lakes, warehouses, and databases
  • AI Infrastructure: Invest in scalable AI infrastructure (cloud and/or on-premises)
  • Tooling: Select tools for data processing, analytics, and AI development
  • Scalability & Agility: Ensure solutions can grow with business needs

Data Governance & Management

  • Data Quality: Ensure accuracy, completeness, and consistency, supported by data contracts
  • Governance Framework: Define ownership, stewardship, and compliance policies
  • Lifecycle Management: Manage data creation, storage, retention, archiving, and deletion
  • Classification & Integration: Categorize data by sensitivity/value and integrate from diverse sources
  • Security & Privacy: Implement encryption, access controls, masking, and compliance with regulations

Responsible AI & Ethics

  • Ethical Principles: Fairness, transparency, accountability, and positive impact
  • Bias Mitigation: Detect and address bias in data and models
  • Security: Safeguard against misuse, leaks, and breaches
  • Monitoring & Governance: Track KPIs for performance, cost, and fairness
  • Guiding Principles: Helpful, Secure, Monitored, and Governed

Analytics & Insights

  • Advanced Analytics: Apply predictive and prescriptive methods to generate insights
  • Visualization: Communicate insights effectively through clear visualizations

Integration & Adoption

  • Operationalization: Embed insights into business processes
  • Change Management: Drive cultural transformation and user adoption

Performance & Optimization

  • KPIs: Define metrics for tracking data and AI impact
  • Continuous Improvement: Iterate and refine based on feedback

Innovation & Future Readiness

  • R&D: Invest in emerging technologies and methodologies
  • Experimentation: Foster a culture of testing and learning

Talent & Culture

  • Expertise: Build teams with the right skills
  • Training: Upskill employees to work with data and AI
  • Cultural Shift: Promote data-driven decision-making

Evaluating Technology

A technology evaluation is crucial for making informed decisions that minimize risks and maximize returns. It ensures alignment with business needs, avoids costly mistakes by identifying potential pitfalls early on, and facilitates the selection of a solution that is not only effective but also scalable, secure, and cost-efficient.

Show More ▼

Part 1: Defining the Need and Initial Research

  • Problem Statement Documentation: Clearly articulate the problem the new technology aims to solve. Justify why existing solutions are inadequate.
  • Technology Research: Investigate at least three potential technologies. For each, document:
    • Cost Analysis: Estimate current and future costs
    • Robustness and Error Handling: Evaluate how the technology handles errors and maintains data integrity
    • Security: Evaluate security features and compliance certifications
    • Scalability: Determine how the technology scales with increasing data volume
    • Disaster Recovery: Outline the technology's disaster recovery plan
    • Vendor Support & SLA: Investigate level of vendor support available
    • Maintenance: Consider long-term maintenance costs and effort
    • Suitability Assessment: Analyze how well the technology fits the specific use case
    • Integration: Assess ease of integrating with existing systems
    • Expertise and Learning Curve: Assess whether in-house expertise exists

Part 2: Recommendation & Proof of Concept

  • Recommendation: Based on the research, recommend a technology and secure agreement from key stakeholders.
  • Proof of Concept (POC): Conduct a POC to validate the chosen technology with clear goals and thorough documentation of methodology, data used, and results.
  • Documentation of Findings: Document both positive and negative findings from the POC.

Part 3: Iteration & Final Decision

  • Issue Resolution: If the POC reveals significant issues, either select a different technology or revise the evaluation criteria. Repeat steps in Phase 1 and 2.
  • Approval and Implementation: If the POC is successful, share the results with stakeholders and obtain approval before proceeding with full implementation.

Project Planning & Prioritization

Project planning and prioritization is crucial for focusing team efforts, optimizing resource allocation, and maximizing impact. The value-complexity matrix enhances this process by providing a visual, systematic assessment of projects based on value and complexity.

Show More ▼

Key Components

  • Problem Statement / Hypothesis: A concise description of the issue or assumption to be addressed.
  • Why it Matters: An explanation of the problem's significance, its potential effects, and the reasons for addressing it.
    • Frequency Rating (1-5, 5 highest): How often the problem occurs
    • Impact Rating (1-5, 5 highest): Severity or consequence of the problem
  • Solution Summary: A brief overview of the proposed solution.
  • Value Score (1-5): A numerical representation of the solution's overall value and potential impact.
  • Complexity / Effort Score (1-5): A numerical rating reflecting the level of difficulty and resources required.
  • Matrix Placement: The location of the project on the value-complexity matrix based on scores.

Example: Route Optimization AI

  • Problem Statement: Frequent and significant deviations from planned delivery routes result in increased fuel costs, extended delivery times, and reduced overall logistical efficiency.
  • Why it Matters: Optimizing delivery routes directly impacts profitability by reducing fuel consumption (estimated savings of $50,000-$100,000 annually), improving on-time delivery performance (reducing late deliveries by 15-20%), and increasing the number of deliveries possible per day (increasing delivery capacity by 10-15%).
    • Frequency Rating: 4
    • Impact Rating: 4
  • Solution Summary: Develop a route optimization AI that analyzes real-time traffic data, weather conditions, and historical delivery patterns to predict and adjust routes dynamically, minimizing deviations.
  • Value Score: 4
  • Complexity / Effort Score: 4
  • Matrix Placement: Strategic Initiative

Vision & Goal Development

Building a vision and setting measurable goals for a data and AI team is crucial because it provides clear direction, aligns efforts with broader business objectives, and fosters a sense of purpose and focus. This framework uses Simon Sinek's Golden Circle: Why, How, and What.

Show More ▼

The Golden Circle Framework

  • Why: This represents the purpose, cause, or belief—the reason an organization or individual exists beyond making money.
  • How: This explains the processes, values, or unique approaches that enable the "Why" to be realized.
  • What: This refers to the products, services, or outcomes an organization delivers.

Example Implementation

Our "Why" (Vision)

We believe that enabling the power of data and AI helps us reduce friction and provide actionable insights for our internal teams and customers, increase customer ROI, and create new revenue streams.

"How" We Will Win (Theme)

  • Continuous Experimentation: By running quick, continuous, and incremental experiments, we will optimize engineering cycles and prove or disprove high value outcomes quickly.
  • Enable & Drive Self-Service Analytics: By enabling and driving analytics self-service through tools like ThoughtSpot, we will open the door to focus on more complex and higher value initiatives.
  • Deliver High Value Outcomes: By delivering measurable and high value outcomes, we will show how analytics as a function can impact the business in a powerful way.
  • Move Up the Analytics Maturity Curve: By shifting analysis focus from mainly descriptive to the proactive full cycle of descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and prescriptive, we will reduce analysis cycle time and empower our teams.

"What" We Will Do (Goals)

  • Run at least 12 experiments this year to validate or invalidate high-value data and AI outcomes.
    • Success Measure: Implement 3 successful experiment outcomes into production.
  • Drive adoption of self-service analytics among business teams to reduce reliance on the data team for routine analytics.
    • Success Measure: Reduce ad-hoc analytics requests by 30%.
  • Complete 5 data and AI initiatives that directly impact cost savings or revenue generation.
    • Success Measure: Generate $1 million in measurable business value.
  • Shift 50% of analytics projects to include predictive or prescriptive insights.
    • Success Measure: Reduce analysis cycle time by 25% on transitioned projects.

Timing is Important

When to act is just as important as what to do. Organizations shouldn't expect to build out every component of a data and AI journey at once. Instead, success comes from starting small, iterating, and scaling as business needs evolve.

Show More ▼

Key Principles

  • Strategy First, But Lightweight: Define a clear vision, objectives, and initial use cases early, but keep the strategy pragmatic. The goal is to create direction, not a rigid plan.
  • Build vs. Buy Decisions: Early on, lean toward buying tools or leveraging managed services to move quickly. As complexity and scale increase, reassess where building custom capabilities provides strategic advantage.
  • Team Formation: Start with a small, multifunctional team or outsourced expertise. Establishing a centralized CoE or hub-and-spoke model is better suited once the organization reaches a level of complexity.
  • Infrastructure Evolution: Many new organizations start with fragmented or ad-hoc data infrastructure. Consolidation should begin in the growth stage to unify systems and reduce silos, with enterprise-scale platforms introduced as the organization matures.
  • Governance & Ethics: Begin with lightweight governance and privacy practices that match current scale. Formal governance frameworks and Responsible AI policies can expand as data volumes and model use grow.
  • Iterate With Business Growth: Each phase of business expansion (new products, new markets, regulatory requirements) should trigger a reassessment of strategy, infrastructure, and team structure. Timing is not a one-time decision but an ongoing adjustment.
